# X Thread Skill — Viral Thread Patterns

Injected into the calendar's thread generation prompt to produce threads that get bookmarks, reposts, and followers — not just likes.

## The Problem With Generic Threads

Generic threads ("Hot takes about AI") get likes from people who already follow you. They don't get bookmarks (save-worthy), reposts (share-worthy), or profile visits (follow-worthy). The difference is specificity and structure.

## What Makes Threads Go Viral

### The Three Signals That Matter
1. **Bookmarks** = "I need to come back to this" → saves = 10x a like in algorithm weight
2. **Reposts** = "My audience needs to see this" → reposts = 20x a like
3. **Profile visits** = "Who is this person?" → the only signal that converts to followers

### What Triggers Each Signal
- Bookmarks: actionable frameworks, tools lists, step-by-step processes, templates
- Reposts: contrarian takes with proof, surprising data, "I wish I'd known this" insights
- Profile visits: expertise demonstration, personality, "this person knows things I don't"

## Thread Structure That Converts

### The Hook Tweet (Tweet 1) — EVERYTHING depends on this

The hook must do THREE things in under 270 characters:
1. **Pattern interrupt** — stop the scroll with something unexpected
2. **Qualify the reader** — "this is for YOU specifically"  
3. **Promise value** — make the payoff clear

### 7 Hook Formulas That Actually Work

**1. The Specific Number**
"I analyzed 500 cold emails that got replies. Here's what the top 1% do differently:"
WHY IT WORKS: Specific number = credibility. "Analyzed" = work you did that I didn't.

**2. The Transformation**
"6 months ago I had 200 followers and 0 clients. Today: 12K followers, 6-figure pipeline. The exact playbook:"
WHY IT WORKS: Before/after with specific numbers. Reader thinks "I want that transformation."

**3. The Contrarian Claim**
"Unpopular opinion: daily posting is killing your X growth. Here's the math:"
WHY IT WORKS: Challenges something the reader believes. They HAVE to click to see the argument.

**4. The Insider Knowledge**
"I spent 3 years at [Company]. Here's what nobody outside knows about how they actually [do thing]:"
WHY IT WORKS: Access to information the reader can't get elsewhere.

**5. The Steal-My-System**
"My exact system for [desirable outcome] (took me 2 years to build, yours in 5 minutes):"
WHY IT WORKS: Asymmetric value exchange. Years of work → 5 minute thread. Instant bookmark.

**6. The Mistake Thread**
"$50,000 in mistakes so you don't have to. 7 things I'd do differently building my startup:"
WHY IT WORKS: People learn more from failures. Specific dollar amount = real consequences.

**7. The Curated List**
"9 free tools that replaced my $2,000/month tech stack:"
WHY IT WORKS: Immediate utility. Specific savings number. Bookmarked for later use.

### HOOKS THAT FAIL
- "Thread on [topic]" — no curiosity gap
- "Let's talk about [topic]" — no value promise
- "I've been thinking about [topic]" — nobody cares what you think without a reason to care
- "[Topic] is important. Here's why:" — the "high school essay" opener
- Any hook without a specific number, timeframe, or outcome

## Body Tweets (Tweets 2-6) — The Curiosity Chain

### The Rule: Each Tweet Must Do Two Things
1. Deliver one complete insight (valuable on its own if someone only sees this tweet)
2. Create a reason to read the next tweet (open loop, partial reveal, "but here's the catch")

### Tactics Per Tweet

**Tweet 2: Establish credibility**
Share the specific context that qualifies you to write this. "I've been doing X for Y years" or "After testing this with Z clients."

**Tweets 3-5: One insight per tweet**
- Lead with the insight, not the setup: "The #1 mistake: [mistake]" not "Many people make mistakes. One common one is..."
- Use concrete numbers: "This doubled our conversion from 2% to 4.1%" not "This improved our conversion significantly"
- Use the "you" voice: "You're probably doing X. Stop. Here's why:" — direct address
- Alternate formats: one tweet = numbered insight, next tweet = short story, next = data point
- Each tweet under 240 chars for maximum readability

**Format tricks that increase engagement:**
- Single-word lines for emphasis: "Wrong." / "Simple." / "Wild."
- Parenthetical asides: "(most people skip this part)"
- "Here's the thing:" on its own line before the insight
- "But." as a tweet opener — creates a pivot

### The CTA Tweet (Final Tweet)

Two options that work:
1. **The question**: "Which one resonated most? Drop it below." → drives replies (13.5x like weight)
2. **The repost ask**: "Bookmark this. Repost it. Someone in your timeline needs it." → drives reposts (20x)

DO NOT end with: "Follow me for more" (desperate), a link (algorithm penalty), or nothing (missed opportunity).

## Thread Types Ranked by Follower Conversion

1. **"Steal my system" threads** — highest follower conversion. You give away something that took you years to build. Reader thinks "if the free stuff is this good..."
2. **Mistake/failure threads** — second highest. Vulnerability + specific lessons. People follow for future lessons.
3. **Curated list threads** — highest bookmarks but moderate follows. Utility without personality.
4. **Data analysis threads** — high reposts. "I analyzed X and found Y" — the effort impresses.
5. **Hot take threads** — highest likes, LOWEST follower conversion. Opinions don't demonstrate expertise.

## What NOT to Generate

NEVER generate threads that are:
- Generic commentary on a trending topic without specific data or personal experience
- "Let's talk about [topic]" opinion pieces with no actionable takeaway
- Threads where every tweet is a different hot take (no narrative arc)
- Threads with no specific numbers, examples, or frameworks
- Threads that could have been written by anyone — if the reader can't tell WHO wrote it, it won't convert to follows

## The Specificity Test

Before outputting a thread, check: does every body tweet contain at least ONE of:
- A specific number (%, $, timeframe, count)
- A named tool, person, or company
- A concrete "do this, not that" instruction
- A personal anecdote with specific details

If a tweet has none of these, it's generic. Rewrite it.

## Thread Length

- 4-6 tweets: optimal for most topics. Tight, bookmarkable.
- 7-10 tweets: only for "steal my system" or curated list threads that need more space.
- 3 or fewer: not a thread. It's a tweet storm. Doesn't get the thread algorithm boost.
- 11+: most readers drop off. Only if every tweet is independently valuable.

## Adapting to Topic Type

### Topic: Trending news (e.g., "AI agents")
DON'T: Generic hot takes about AI. Everyone is doing that.
DO: "I've been building AI agents for 6 months. 3 things the discourse gets completely wrong:" — specific experience + contrarian angle.

### Topic: Technical/how-to (e.g., "Claude API")
DON'T: Tutorial thread that reads like documentation.
DO: "I wasted 40 hours on Claude API edge cases so you don't have to. The 5 gotchas nobody warns you about:" — pain avoidance + specific number.

### Topic: Industry trend (e.g., "startup fundraising")
DON'T: "Fundraising is changing. Here's how."
DO: "I raised $2M in 3 weeks using a strategy most VCs hate. The exact email I sent (and why 80% responded):" — specific outcome + curiosity gap.
