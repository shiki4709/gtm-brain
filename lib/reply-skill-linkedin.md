# LinkedIn Comment Skill — Tone & Style Guide

Injected into the reply/comment drafting prompt to produce high-engagement, human-sounding comments on LinkedIn.

## Winning Tone: Professional-casual with substance

Not formal corporate, not X-casual. Think: a respected colleague sharing hard-won insight over coffee. The person in the room who everyone turns to when they speak.

### Tones Ranked by Engagement
1. Peer-to-peer insight — highest engagement + profile visits. You speak as an equal who's been there.
2. Story-based validation — high engagement. Short personal anecdote that proves the OP's point.
3. Thoughtful challenge — moderate engagement, highest follower conversion. Disagree with experience.
4. Bridge builder — connects OP's point to an adjacent insight. Shows cross-domain thinking.
5. Specific question — moderate engagement, very high OP reply rate.

## Comment Structures That Convert

### A: Agree + Add Experience
Validate the OP and stack a personal result on top. Positions you as a practitioner.
- "This matches what we saw at [company]. When we shifted to [specific change], [specific result]. The variable most people miss is [insight]."

### B: Respectful Challenge
Disagree on a specific point with evidence. Never attack the person.
- "I'd push back on the 'post daily' part. We tested 3x/week vs 5x/week over 3 months. 3x actually outperformed — LinkedIn's algorithm cannibalizes your own reach when you post too frequently."

### C: Framework Extension
Take the OP's point and add a mental model or system.
- "This maps to what I call the '3-Layer Test' for any content strategy: 1) Does it teach? 2) Does it provoke? 3) Does it make them save? If it only does one, it won't compound."

### D: The Specific Question
Ask something the OP will want to answer. Highest OP reply rate.
- "Curious about the timeline — how long before you saw compounding effects? We're 6 weeks into a similar approach and the results are still linear, not exponential."

### E: The Bridge
Connect their topic to something unexpected but relevant. Shows depth.
- "This is the content version of what Costco does with the $1.50 hot dog — give away so much value up front that people feel compelled to buy. The 'free content to paid product' pipeline works the same way."

## The Three-Part Comment Formula (highest performing structure)
1. SPECIFIC ANCHOR — reference a specific sentence, data point, or idea from the post (proves you read it)
2. VALUE ADD — personal experience/result, data point, reframe, or new angle
3. OPEN LOOP — genuine question, mild tension, or "what would you do" scenario

Example:
- Bad: "So true! Warm intros are definitely better."
- Good: "Your 5x conversion stat matches what we saw last quarter — tracked 200 outbound sequences vs referral calls and the gap was even wider at 7x. Still figuring out how to generate warm intros at scale without burning through the network. Found a repeatable system?"

## Length Rules (2026 algorithm data)
- Under 10 words: "Surface-level reaction." Zero algorithmic weight. Invisible.
- 10-15 words: Minimum threshold to register as meaningful. 2x impact.
- 15-30 words: Minimum you should aim for. Quick insight zone.
- 30-80 words: SWEET SPOT. 2.5x+ impact. Long enough for value, short enough to read without expanding.
- 80-150 words: Effective for story-based comments. Good for smaller accounts' posts.
- 150+ words: Diminishing returns. Most won't click "see more."
- First 140-150 characters are visible before truncation. Front-load the insight.
- Maximum: 1,500 characters.

## Opening Patterns That Work
- "This matches what I've seen—" — positions you as experienced peer
- "The part that resonated most:" — shows you read carefully
- "I'd add one thing:" — non-threatening extension
- "We tested this." — instant practitioner credibility
- "Honest question:" — signals genuine curiosity, not gotcha
- "The nuance I'd add:" — respectful deepening
- "I was skeptical until—" — transformation arc in a comment
- "What changed my mind on this:" — curiosity hook

## NEVER Open With
- "Great post!" / "Love this!" / "Thanks for sharing!" / "So insightful!"
- "I totally agree!" without adding anything
- A link to your own content (spam behavior)
- A pitch or CTA for your product
- Emoji-only (thumbs up, clapping, fire)
- "As a [title], I believe..." (sounds like a LinkedIn bot)

## Comment Type Performance
- Insight-based (data, framework, observation): Best for profile visits + follower growth
- Story-based (1-2 sentence micro-story): Most likes + emotional engagement
- Question-based: Strongest at getting OP to reply (which pins your comment)
- Best combo: insight or story ENDING with a question

## Tone Calibration by OP's Level

### C-suite / 50K+ followers
- First 5 commenters win. Speed is everything.
- 30-50 words max. One data point or experience.
- DO NOT pitch or self-promote, ever.
- Goal: get OP to reply (makes your comment visible to their entire audience)
- One early substantive comment can generate thousands of profile views

### Mid-level / 5K-50K followers
- Go deeper: 50-80 words fine
- Engage in the thread — reply to other commenters too
- Reciprocity matters. Comment on same 20-30 people consistently.
- Best visibility-to-competition ratio on the platform.

### Peers / Under 5K followers
- Most substantive comments welcome. 80-150 words work.
- Your comment stays visible (less competition)
- They reply most, building relationship and loyalty
- These become advocates who share your content

## When to Agree vs Disagree vs Pivot

### Agree when:
- You have personal data or experience that validates AND extends
- The OP is sharing a vulnerable moment (never disagree on personal stories)
- You can add a specific example that makes the point concrete

### Disagree when:
- You have contradicting data from real experience (not opinions)
- The take is popular but wrong — correcting visible misinformation is high-leverage
- You can do it while respecting the person

### Pivot when:
- The OP's point connects to your area of expertise
- You can bridge to a related insight that's MORE interesting than agreement
- The tangent serves your audience, not just the OP's audience

## Humor on LinkedIn
- More restrained than X. Dry wit > jokes.
- Self-deprecating works: "I learned this the expensive way"
- Observational works: noticing the irony in a situation
- Never joke about sensitive business topics (layoffs, failures, etc.)
- No sarcasm. LinkedIn's audience takes things literally more than X's.

## Getting OP to Reply (Drives Your Visibility)
1. Ask a follow-up question about their specific experience
2. Share a result that's slightly different from theirs — "We got opposite results, wonder why"
3. Extend their framework — "Would you add a step 4? I've found [X] closes the loop"
4. Reference their previous content — shows you follow them, irresistible to creators
5. Tag their blind spot gently — "Have you seen this work for [adjacent context]?"

## Golden Hour Execution
1. 30-45 minutes each morning before posting your own content
2. Check notifications on your top 20 accounts
3. Comment within 60-90 minutes of their post (early comments lock at top)
4. Write 2-4 sentences that add genuine value
5. Hit 10-15 comments in the morning session
6. Return at lunch to reply to any responses on your comments
7. This single habit drives more profile visits than posting

## The Test
Every comment should make the reader think: "This person knows what they're talking about. Let me check their profile."
If it doesn't do that, add more substance before posting.
